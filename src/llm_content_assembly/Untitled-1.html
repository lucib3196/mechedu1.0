Summary of the Lecture

This lecture focuses on the concept of specific heat capacity, which is the amount of heat energy required to raise the temperature of 1 kg of a substance by 1°C. It explains the formula for calculating the heat energy required for temperature changes and provides examples with different substances. The lecture also includes problem-solving exercises to apply the concept of specific heat capacity in practical scenarios.

  • Specific heat capacity is denoted by $c$ and measured in J/(kg °C).
  • The formula $Q = mc(t_2 - t_1)$ is used to calculate the heat energy $Q$ required to change the temperature of a mass $m$ from $t_1$ to $t_2$.
  • Examples are provided to calculate the heat energy required or lost in changing the temperature of various substances, using their specific heat capacities.

Specific Heat Capacity Formula Derivation

Derivation of Specific Heat Capacity Formula

Step-by-Step Derivation

  1. Step 1: Define Specific Heat Capacity

    The specific heat capacity of a substance is the quantity of heat energy required to raise the temperature of 1 kg of the substance by 1°C.

  2. Step 2: Understand the Formula

    The formula to calculate the heat energy required to raise the temperature of a mass $m$ of a substance with a specific heat capacity $c$ from temperature $t_1$ to $t_2$ is given by:

    $$ Q = mc(t_2 - t_1) $$

  3. Step 3: Explain Variables

    In this formula:

    • Q is the heat energy (in joules)
    • m is the mass (in kg)
    • c is the specific heat capacity (in J/kg°C)
    • t_1 is the initial temperature (in °C)
    • t_2 is the final temperature (in °C)

  4. Final Note

    This formula is fundamental in thermodynamics and helps understand how different materials absorb and transfer heat.

Problem 4: Heat Lost by Cast Iron

Derivation of Heat Lost by Cast Iron

Step-by-Step Derivation

  1. Step 1: Identify the formula for heat loss

    The formula for calculating heat loss is given by:

    $$ Q = mc(t_2 - t_1) $$

    Where:

    • Q is the heat lost
    • m is the mass of the substance (in kg)
    • c is the specific heat capacity (in J/(kg °C))
    • t_1 is the initial temperature (in °C)
    • t_2 is the final temperature (in °C)

  2. Step 2: Substitute the given values into the formula

    Now, substitute m = 10 kg, c = 500 J/(kg °C), t_1 = 150 °C, and t_2 = 50 °C into the formula:

    $$ Q = 10 /times 500 \times (50 - 150) $$

  3. Step 3: Calculate the result

    Now perform the calculation:

    $$ Q = 10 imes 500 imes (-100) $$

  4. Final Result:

    The result gives us:

    $$ Q = -500000 ext{ J or } -500 ext{ kJ or } -0.5 ext{ MJ} $$

Problem 5: Mass of Lead from Heat Energy

Derivation of Mass of Lead from Heat Energy

Step-by-Step Derivation

  1. Step 1: Heat energy equation

    To determine the mass of lead, we start with the formula for heat energy:

    $$ Q = mc(t_2 - t_1) $$

    Where:

    • Q is the heat energy absorbed (in Joules)
    • m is the mass (in kg)
    • c is the specific heat capacity (in J/(kg °C))
    • t_1 is the initial temperature (in °C)
    • t_2 is the final temperature (in °C)

    This equation allows us to relate the heat energy absorbed to the mass of the substance and its temperature change.

  2. Step 2: Rearranging for mass

    To isolate mass, we rearrange the equation as follows:

    $$ m = \frac{Q}{c(t_2 - t_1)} $$

  3. Step 3: Substitute the values

    Now we will substitute the provided values into the formula. Here, the quantity of heat required is 780 kJ, which needs to be converted into Joules (1 kJ = 1000 J):

    $$ Q = 780 \text{ kJ} = 780000 \text{ J} $$

    The specific heat capacity of lead is given as 130 J/(kg °C), and the temperatures are 27 °C (initial) and 327 °C (final). Therefore, we substitute:

    $$ m = \frac{780000}{130 \times (327 - 27)} $$

  4. Step 4: Calculate the mass

    Next, we will compute the value:

    Calculating the denominator:

    $$ 130 \times (327 - 27) = 130 \times 300 = 39000 $$

    Thus, substituting back, we have:

    $$ m = \frac{780000}{39000} \approx 20 \text{ kg} $$
