Summary of Lecture

The lecture covers the properties of fluids, focusing on density, the ideal gas law, bulk modulus, speed of sound in a medium, surface tension, and capillary action. It begins with the concept of density and its application to different substances. The ideal gas law is introduced, explaining the relationship between pressure, volume, and temperature for gases. The bulk modulus and its role in determining the speed of sound in fluids are discussed. Surface tension is explored, including its effects on pressure and force. Finally, capillary action is explained, highlighting the balance of forces involved.

Key Concepts

  • Density: Defined as mass per unit volume, with examples for water, mercury, and gasoline.
  • Ideal Gas Law: Describes the relationship $PV = n\bar{R}T$ for gases, where $P$ is pressure, $V$ is volume, $n$ is the number of moles, $\bar{R}$ is the universal gas constant, and $T$ is temperature.
  • Bulk Modulus: A measure of a fluid's resistance to compression, defined as $K = -\frac{dP}{dV/V}$, where $dP$ is the change in pressure and $dV/V$ is the relative change in volume.
  • Speed of Sound: In a fluid, it is determined by $c = \sqrt{\frac{K}{\rho}}$, where $K$ is the bulk modulus and $\rho$ is the density.
  • Surface Tension: The force per unit length acting along the surface of a liquid, affecting pressure as $p = \frac{2\sigma}{R}$, where $\sigma$ is the surface tension and $R$ is the radius.
  • Capillary Action: The movement of liquid within a narrow space due to surface tension and adhesive forces, described by $h = \frac{2\sigma \cos \theta}{\rho g R}$, where $h$ is the height, $\theta$ is the contact angle, $\rho$ is the density, $g$ is the gravitational acceleration, and $R$ is the radius of the tube.

Derivations

Density and Ideal Gas Law

Density is defined as mass per unit volume.

  1. \( \rho = \frac{m}{V} \)

    For gases, the ideal gas law is used: \( PV = n\bar{R}T \), where \( n \) is the number of moles and \( \bar{R} \) is the universal gas constant.

  2. \( PV = n\bar{R}T \)

    Expressing the number of moles \( n \) in terms of mass \( m \) and molar mass \( M \), we have \( n = \frac{m}{M} \).

  3. \( PV = \frac{m}{M}\bar{R}T \)

    Rearranging gives \( P = \frac{m}{V}\frac{\bar{R}}{M}T \). Since \( \rho = \frac{m}{V} \), we have \( P = \rho \frac{\bar{R}}{M}T \).

  4. \( P = \rho \frac{\bar{R}}{M}T \)

    For air at 30°C, using \( \bar{R} = 8.314 \) J/(mol·K) and \( M_{\text{air}} \approx 29 \) g/mol, the specific gas constant \( R = \frac{\bar{R}}{M} \approx 0.287 \) kJ/(kg·K).

  5. \( R = 0.287 \text{ kJ/(kg·K)} \)

    Example calculation for air at 30°C: \( P = 101.3 \times 10^3 \) Pa, \( T = 303 \) K, \( \rho = \frac{P}{RT} \).

  6. \( \rho = 1.165 \text{ kg/m}^3 \)

Derivations

Bulk Modulus and Speed of Sound

Bulk modulus \( K \) is defined as \( K = -\frac{\Delta P}{\frac{\Delta V}{V}} \).

  1. \( K = -\frac{\Delta P}{\frac{\Delta V}{V}} \)
  2. The speed of sound in a medium is given by \( c = \sqrt{\frac{K}{\rho}} \).
  3. For water, \( K_{\text{H}_2\text{O}} = 2.1 \) GPa and \( \rho = 1000 \) kg/m³, calculate \( c \).
  4. \( c = \sqrt{\frac{2.1 \times 10^9}{1000}} = 1450 \text{ m/s} \)

Derivations

Speed of Sound in Air

  1. Assume isothermal process: $P = \rho RT$, $c = \sqrt{\frac{dP}{d\rho}}$.
  2. $c = \sqrt{RT}$
  3. Assume adiabatic process: $P = c_2 \rho^\gamma$, $c = \sqrt{\gamma RT}$.
  4. $c = \sqrt{\gamma RT}$
  5. Example calculation for air: $c = \sqrt{1.4 \times 287 \times 303} = 348.9 \text{ m/s}$.
  6. $c = 348.9 \text{ m/s}$
  7. Mach number $Ma = \frac{V}{c}$. If $Ma < 0.3$, assume medium is incompressible; if $Ma > 1$, supersonic flow.
  8. $Ma = \frac{V}{c}$

Derivations

Surface Tension and Pressure

Surface tension force is $\sigma$ with units N/m. Force due to pressure is $P\pi R^2$.

  1. $P\pi R^2$
  2. Force due to surface tension is $\sigma (2\pi R)$. Equating forces gives $P\pi R^2 = \sigma (2\pi R)$.
  3. $P = \frac{2\sigma}{R}$

Derivations

Capillary Action

  1. Upward force due to surface tension is $\sigma (2\pi R) \cos \theta$.

    \( \sigma (2\pi R) \cos \theta \)

  2. Downward force due to gravity is $\rho g \pi R^2 h$.

    \( \rho g \pi R^2 h \)

  3. Balancing forces gives $\sigma (2\pi R) \cos \theta = \rho g \pi R^2 h$.

    \( h = \frac{2\sigma \cos \theta}{\rho g R} \)

  4. $\theta$ is the wetting angle, with different behaviors for $0 < \theta < 90$ and $90 < \theta < 180$.

    \( \theta \) is the wetting angle
